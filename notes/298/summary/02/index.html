Time Value of Money

Whenever we predict a future cash flow, we have to discount that cash flow back to the present so that it is meaningful for comparisons.

The cash flow for a bond is interest, and the cash flow for a stock are dividends.

Calculating Present Value

The general formula for the present value of a sum of money is:

PV = FV / (1 + r)N

Where:

  • PV — present value
  • FV — future value
  • r — rate of return (depends on stock/bonds/etc)
  • N — compounding periods

For example, let's say that a person leaves $10,000 invested for 5 years. At the end of the five years, the account is worth $17,000.

What was the rate of return?

Essentially we need to solve for r in the previous equation. So we have:

  • PV — $10,000
  • FV — $17,000
  • N — 5

Therefore:

10,000 = 17,000 / (1 + r)5
1+r = (17,000/10,000)0.2
r = 11.2%

Different Compounding Periods

In the previous example compounding periods is equal to the number of years. This is the general assumption.

However it is possible for the rate to be compounded quarterly/monthly/daily as well. In this case, we have to appropriately divide the yearly rate by the compounding period.

For example, if an investment earns 4% / year, but it is compounded quarterly, then the rate that we use is 4 / 4 = 1%.

Effective vs. Stated Rate

The effective rate is the rate that takes into account compounding periods. The stated rate is simply a rate that is stated annually. Whenever the compounding period is less than annual, the effective rate is higher than the stated rate.

The formula is:

ER = (1 + SR/N)N - 1

Where:

  • ER — effective rate
  • SR — standard rate
  • N — compounding periods per year

For example, if an investment has a SR of 18% and is compounded quarterly, the effective rate is:

ER = (1 + (18%/4))4 - 1
ER = 1.0454 - 1 = 19.25%

Ordinary Annuity & Annuity Due

Ordinary annuity refers to when a cash flow is added (or subtracted) at the end of each subperiod.

Annuity due refers to when a cash flow is added at the beginning of each subperiod.

When not specified, the problem refers to ordinary annuity.

The formula for the FV of an ordinary annuity is:

FV = P(((1 + r)n - 1) / r)

Where:

  • FV — future value
  • P — payments
  • r — rate
  • n — compounding periods

For example, if a person adds $7,000 per year to an account for 10 years and he receives 6% per year on the money, what is the account worth at the end of the 10 years?

FV = 7,000(((1.06)10 - 1) / 0.06) = $92,265

Sometimes we are given a final value and we are asked for the minimum payments to be made. In this case we simply alter the formula to become:

P = FV/(((1 + r)n - 1) / r)

Say with a rate of 6%, we want $112,000 in our account after 10 years. What should our payments be?

P = 112,000/((1.0610 - 1) / 0.06) = $8,497

Perpetuity

A perpetuity is a type of payment that is never ending. One example of this is preferred stock.

Since it is never ending, we can only talk about its present value. The formula for that is simply:

PV = P/r

Where:

  • PV — present value
  • P — payments
  • r — rate of return

For example, if a preferred stock of ABC corporation pays $10 per share annual dividend with a rate of return of 5%, the value of the stock is:

PV = $10 / 5% = $200

Unequal Cash Flows

It is possible that the cash flows are uneven (generally in deciding potential projects to undertake). In this case, the PV has to be determined on an individual basis.

The general formula is:

PV = CF0 + CF1/(1+r)1 + ... + CFn/(1+r)n

Consider the following problem:

A company is deciding whether or not to invest in a joint venture. The initial cost is $10 million and the estimated cash flows are:

  • Year 1 — $1,000,000
  • Year 2 — $1,250,000
  • Year 3 — $1,500,000

The firm's interest at the venture can be sold after 3 years for $12 million. If the required rate of return is 15%, what is the investment's net present value?

First we have to find the PV of all the future cash flows.

  • CF0 — -10,000 (10 mill initially invested)
  • CF1 — 1,000/(1.15)1 = 869.565
  • CF2 — 1,250/(1.15)2 = 945.179
  • CF3 — (1,500 + 12,000)/(1.15)3 = 8876.469

The PV is the sum of all future cash flows, therefore:

-10,000 + 869.565 + 945.179 + 8876.469 = $691,214
